Bigo Bridge



Photo taken March 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Tucked deep within the lush silence of Sitio Imbargo, Barangay Bigo, stands a bridge that doesn’t just span a river—it spans centuries. Modest at 24 feet long and 20 feet wide, the Bigo Bridge is built like a fortress. Crafted during the Spanish era from perfectly cut adobe stones engraved with Roman numerals, its foundation is a mix of basalt, lime, and—believe it or not—honey (pulot), all laid down through the brutal system of polo y servicio (forced labor). Now, moss creeps across its ancient walls, and thick foliage—alagao, tibig, bamboo, takip-asin—nearly conceals it from sight. Rusted railings and solar lights hint at modern attempts to reclaim it, but the air still hangs heavy with the echoes of the past.

🌟 Fun Fact: Locals say early churchgoers at St. Catherine of Alexandria were required to bring a stone to Sunday mass—or endure 25 lashes. No stone, no salvation.

👻 Myths from the Mossy Stones : Legends swirl around Bigo Bridge. A glowing white cross that vanishes upon approach. Shadowy figures glimpsed just before sunrise. Even santelmo—mysterious balls of fire—are said to drift through the trees, sometimes hitching rides with passing motorists. And if your radio suddenly goes static mid-crossing? Locals will simply nod. "You're not the first."



Location: Bigo Bridge

Calibuyboy Bridge



Photo taken November 2021
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Calibuyboy Bridge may be just over 5 meters long, but its history runs deep. Built in the 1870s through forced labor, it once formed part of Calle Real—the old Spanish highway used for trade and governance. Made of adobe and lime, the bridge now lies partly hidden under tree roots and legends. Locals whisper of a tikbalang, a ghostly white lady, and a pig-like boy said to roam its path at night.

🌟 Fun Fact: Spanish traveler Juan Alvarez Guerra crossed this very bridge in the 1800s and mentioned it in Viajes por Filipinas, capturing a rare glimpse of life during colonial rule.



Location: Calibuyboy Bridge

Iyakin Bridge



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Nestled quietly between Tayabas and Pagbilao, Iyakin Bridge may be small, but it carries the weight of centuries. Built in the 1870s through forced labor, it was once part of Calle Real, the Spanish trade route connecting towns. Made from adobe, lime, and molasses, the bridge still supports light vehicles crossing a creek steeped in history and myth—where Japanese troops once marched, guerrillas hid, and locals say cries from the river gave the bridge its haunting name.

🌟 Fun Fact: Some historians call it Puente de Juan, believing Spanish writer Juan Alvarez Guerra may have crossed it during his travels, immortalizing it not just in stone—but in story.



Location: Iyakin Bridge

Tulay ng Salak



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Tucked in Sitio Pulang Lupa, Tulay ng Salak may be small, but it once played a big role—linking Quezon Province to Bicol during the American colonial era. This 8.94-meter concrete beam bridge served as a lifeline for farmers, students, and merchants crossing between barangays. Though now cracked and worn, it still carries light vehicles and echoes of old stories—from a ghostly boy in white to tales of hidden Spanish treasure.

🌟 Fun Fact: Locals say a tractor once broke its old barriers, and during floods, it remains the only gravel path connecting two communities—keeping both lives and legends flowing.



Location: Tulay ng Salak

Baywalk Daungan (Pantalan)



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Right in front of Daungan’s Barangay Hall lies Pantalan ng Pagbilao a 103-meter concrete port that’s more than just a docking site; it’s a living piece of local culture. What started in the 1950s as a floating balsa has since evolved into a vibrant hub where fishermen unload their catch, bangkeros rest, and neighbors gather beside the baywalk lined with palms and stalls. From trade and travel to fluvial parades and seafaring blessings during Araw ng Pagbilao, the port remains a place where stories flow like the tide.

🌟 Fun Fact: This port once floated with the waves—and today, it still carries memories, rituals, and even the occasional ghost story.



Location: Baywalk Daungan (Pantalan)

Palsabangon Bridge



Photo taken Year 2021
by Errol Paguio - ( CCTV Supervisor of LGU Pagbilao)

Crossing Brgy. Ibabang Palsabangon, the 60-meter Palsabangon Bridge may seem like an ordinary stretch of concrete—but it holds a heroic past. Built atop the ruins of a wooden bridge blown up by American troops on Christmas Day 1941 to stall advancing Japanese forces, it once echoed with the thunder of dynamite and retreating footsteps. Locals like Mr. Tiburcio Rubico still recall the fiery blast that lit up the sky. Today, it quietly connects travelers and traders along the Bicol highway, while memories of wartime valor, childhood antics, and market-bound students still echo beneath its steel beams.

🌟 Fun Fact: On Christmas Day, 1941, American troops blew up the original Palsabangon Bridge under Japanese fire to help Filipino soldiers escape—lighting up the night with a blast that locals like Mr. Tiburcio Rubico still remember. Where war once raged, kids later slid across planks, students hauled bananas, and boys snuck peeks at river bathers. From battleground to memory lane, the bridge now carries more than just traffic—it carries history.



Location: Palsabangon Bridge

Portes Brother's Cockpit Arena



Photo taken October 2021
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Tucked along the Tayabas–Pagbilao Road, the Portes Brothers’ Cockpit Arena is a fading yet powerful relic of local tradition. Once alive with the roar of betters and the clash of blades, this arena—surrounded by bamboo fences and crowned with a rusting roof—was born in 1972 out of martial law-era improvisation. Originally meant to be a beer house, it evolved into a cultural hub where cockfighting thrived. Inside, tiered wooden bleachers, a steel-framed roweda, and a red ulutan pen once set the stage for high-stakes matches. From anahaw leaves to reinforced steel trusses, the arena grew alongside the community—surviving storms and decades—until the pandemic silenced it in 2020. Today, it stands still but storied, tarps fluttering like memories in the wind.

🌟 Fun Fact: Some locals say the arena was so loud during its heyday, you could hear the crowd from the highway—and during town fiestas, even tricycles would pause just to catch the final match!



Location: Portes Brother's Cockpit Arena

Pagbilao Municipal Building



Photo taken March 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Standing proudly along Bonifacio Street, the Pagbilao Municipal Building has been the heart of local governance since its construction in 1927. Surrounded by iconic landmarks like the Dakilang Kawal Monument and St. Catherine Parish, it has evolved through time—expanding to house offices such as BIR, DSWD, COMELEC, and the Mayor’s Office. Renovated under several administrations, including Hon. Romeo Portes and Mayor Shierre Ann Portes-Palicpic, it now includes auxiliary buildings and a covered court, reflecting Pagbilao’s growing civic needs. Over the years, it has hosted presidents, senators, celebrities, and countless community events. Despite modern upgrades and natural disasters, its original foundation still stands strong—just like the spirit of Pagbilao.

🌟 Fun Fact: In 2002, then-Senator Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo dropped by unannounced—so simple in appearance that people almost didn’t recognize her!



Location: Pagbilao Municipal Building

Pagbilao Public Market



Photo taken January 29 2022
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Built in 1916 and standing strong in Brgy. Del Carmen, the Pamilihang Bayan ng Pagbilao has been the town’s beating heart for over a century. With 120 stalls offering fresh fish, meat, vegetables, and local snacks, it’s more than just a market—it’s a place where stories, friendships, and livelihoods thrive. Surrounded by narra trees, jeepney terminals, and tiangge stalls, it’s a daily stop for locals and visitors alike. From WWII survival to political campaigns and live concerts, the market has seen it all—and it’s still growing!

🌟 Fun Fact: In 1945, after a bombing that destroyed most of Pagbilao, the market was one of the few buildings left standing. In 2018, it even rocked to the sound of Spongecola during Araw ng Pagbilao!



Location: Pagbilao Public Market

Home Economics Building (H.E)



Photo taken September 2021
by Marielle C. Montana

Tucked behind the Supervisor’s Office at Pagbilao Central Elementary School, the Home Economics Building (H.E) built circa 1922—once buzzed with lessons, elections, and local events. With its capiz windows, wooden floors, and ten functional doors, it was both practical and iconic. But time and silence have taken over. Now used for storage, the hall is rich with ghost stories—like phantom cries from its old bathtub and eerie typewriter clicks at night. Despite the dust, its structure remains a testament to its legacy in education and local folklore.

🌟 Fun Fact: Students once played hide-and-seek beneath the hall, only to bolt out in fear—chased, they said, by ghostly shadows!



Location: Home Economics Building (H.E)

Marcos Type Prefabricated Building



Photo taken November 8 2021
by Monika M. Reyes

Tucked behind the mini park of Pagbilao East Elementary School, the Prefab Marcos-Type Building is a colorful 1970s-era classroom built during the Marcos administration to address classroom shortages. With five rooms—nicknamed Crystal, Ruby, Diamond, Sapphire, and Gold—it has served as a hub for Grade 4 classes, athletic quarters, scout camps, and even ghost stories. Its walls, adorned with vibrant murals of animals and Filipino life, make it a standout on campus. Though slated for demolition, it remains a beloved symbol of education, resilience, and imagination.

🌟 Fun Fact: Each classroom is named after a gemstone—adding sparkle and identity to this historic Marcos-era hall!



Location: Marcos Type Prefabricated Building

Saint Anne Parish and Diocesan Shrine



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

The Saint Anne Parish and Diocesan Shrine in Silangang Malicboy, Pagbilao is a historic gem and spiritual heart of the town. Known for its stunning stained-glass facade and five-level bell tower with a spiral staircase, it began as a humble ermita and became a parish in 1958, then a diocesan shrine in 2000. Despite typhoons, war, and termites, it stands strong—hosting sacraments, youth programs, and community events.

🌟 Fun Fact: The bell tower isn’t just for ringing bells—it also hides a storeroom and baptistery inside!



Location: Saint Anne Parish and Diocesan Shrine

St. Catherine of Alexandria Parish



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Right across the Pagbilao Municipal Hall stands the St. Catherine of Alexandria Parish—a stunning blend of faith, history, and heritage. Originally built in 1688 and rebuilt in 1730, this adobe stone church features a majestic 40-foot bell tower with one of Quezon’s oldest bells, dating back to 1784! Its beautifully crafted altar, inspired by Papag at Bilao, reflects the soul of Pagbilao. Despite wars and time, the church remains a vibrant hub for worship and community, especially during the feast of St. Catherine and Holy Week’s Via Crucis.

🌟 Fun Fact: One bell still ringing in the tower has echoed since 1784—older than many towns themselves!



Location: St. Catherine of Alexandria Parish

Pagbilao Old Catholic Cemetery



Photo taken
by LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Tucked between Barangays Mapagong and Castillo, the Pagbilao Old Catholic Cemetery is a three-hectare resting place of the town’s early leaders and beloved figures. Once a swamp, it was transformed in 1912 through a donation by Mayor Segundo Martinez into Pagbilao’s first public cemetery. With its historic tombs, artistic gravestones, and spiritual ambiance, it serves as a site of prayer and remembrance—especially during All Saints’ Day. Though weathered by time, neglect, and ghostly legends, it remains a cultural and historical landmark worth revisiting.

🌟 Fun Fact: The cemetery’s rare three-layered apartment-style tomb continues to intrigue locals—especially during ghost-hunting season!



Location: Pagbilao Old Catholic Cemetery

Bukal ng Itang



Photo taken October 18, 2021
by Rey Ann Magat

Hidden in the muddy fields of Barangay Antipolo, Bukal ng Itang is a humble spring-fed well with roots tracing back to the Spanish era. Dug by a man named Itang, it became a lifeline for locals—offering cool, clean water for drinking, bathing, and laundry long before modern plumbing. Surrounded by folklore, including tales of a nuno sa punso guarding the spring, the well remains a symbol of community resilience and tradition, lovingly maintained by the barangay.

🌟 Fun Fact: In the '70s and '80s, kids bathed in the well before school and left their water jugs behind—ready for pickup after class!



Location: Bukal ng Itang

Goyma Well



Photo taken October 07,2021
by Lourdes Ignacio- Minge

Beside the busy Agora Market in Barangay Sta. Catalina lies a hidden gem—the heritage well built in 1870 by Chinese settler Go Inco Ma. At 20 feet deep and 540 cm around, it’s the largest well in Pagbilao’s town proper, once serving as a lifeline from the Spanish era through the Japanese occupation. Fed by a natural spring and framed by lush plants, it reflects the town’s rich multicultural past and enduring spirit. Now maintained by the barangay and soon to neighbor the future barangay hall, this well continues to flow with both water and history.

🌟 Fun Fact: Even Japanese soldiers once drank from this well—proof that water, like history, connects us all.



Location: Goyma Well

"EME ROAD" Pagbilao Zigzag Road



Photo taken February of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Winding through the Quezon Protected Landscape in Brgy. Silangang Malicboy,EME Road—nicknamed “Bitukang Manok” —is a legendary 7-kilometer zigzag route connecting Manila to Bicol. Built in 1929 and enhanced during the Marcos era, its sharp “siko” turns, steep drops, and breathtaking views make it both scenic and spine-tingling. Once used in wartime and now loved by riders and filmmakers, EME isn’t just a road—it’s a thrilling ride through history, nature, and local lore.

🌟 Fun Fact: There’s a spot on EME where cars mysteriously roll uphill in neutral. Locals call it the “magnet zone”—science or sorcery? You decide.



Location: "EME ROAD" Pagbilao Zigzag Road

PNR Station (Silangang Malicboy)



Photo taken January of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Hidden in the trees of Barangay Silangang Malicboy are the quiet ruins of one of Quezon’s oldest railway stations. Built in 1914, the Silangang Malicboy Station once connected Pagbilao to the Bicol Region via the Manila–Legazpi line. With steam trains powered by carbon and water, it was more than a stop—it was a lifeline, offering jobs, trade, and stories of wartime escapes, presidential visits, and even saintly sightings. Though now overtaken by time and nature, the station’s remnants still echo the heartbeat of a once-thriving community.

🌟 Fun Fact: Others tell of a shadowy figure near the ruined platform—a man in what looks like an old train uniform. He doesn’t speak, only stares, and disappears when approached. Some say he’s the spirit of a former worker who never left his post.



Location: PNR Station (Silangang Malicboy)

Pagbilao Railroad Station



Photo taken January of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Built in 1914, the Pagbilao Railroad Station once linked Manila to Legazpi, serving passengers and goods for over 90 years—until Typhoon Milenyo struck in 2006. Just 40 meters from the Del Carmen public market, this Narra-wood station with its Dutch-style door wasn’t just a transport hub—it became a community space for weddings, birthdays, and even children’s play. Though only 10% of it remains today, the memories live on through local stories and repurposed spaces.

🌟 Fun Fact: Locals recall playing piso-playstations there—and one bride even roasted two goats at the station for her wedding feast!



Location: Pagbilao Railroad Station

Binahaan Dam



Photo taken January of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Tucked in Sitio Logpond, Brgy. Binahaan, Binhaan Dam is a 3.4-meter concrete reservoir built in 1960 by the Southern Luzon Irrigation District. Once made of simple sandbags by farmers, it now irrigates 95 hectares of farmland and supports the local community’s livelihood. With its lush surroundings and cool aqua-green waters, it’s also a go-to spot for swimming, washing, and unwinding.

🌟 Fun Fact: In 1981, a dramatic fight scene from the movie Kamlon starring Bong Revilla Sr. was filmed right on this dam!



Location: Binahaan Dam

Significance


Gudani-Salvanera Ancestral House



Photo taken January of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

In Brgy. Sta. Catalina, Pagbilao, a chocolate brown two-story ancestral home stands as a proud witness to over a century of history. Built in the late 1800s by the Gudani-Salvanera family , it has survived pirate raids, a railroad relocation in 1909, and even occupation as a Japanese garrison during WWII. Today, it's part business space, part family haven—preserving antique furniture, religious icons, and even a rare photo of Jose Rizal’s execution.

🌟 Fun Fact: Once a classroom by day and glowing like Vigan by night, it even housed the image of Virgen La Milagrosa during Flores de Mayo!



Location: Gudani-Salvanera Ancestral House

Gloria Ancestral House



Photo taken January of 2022
by LGU Tourism Pagbilao

Tucked between Tambak River and the sea in Pagbilao’s Wharf area, the Gloria Ancestral House —built in the 1930s by Emilio and Agripina Gloria—blends elegance with hard work. This breezy two-story wooden home, filled with heirlooms and even a piano from New York, has been a hub for political talks, religious events, and lively gatherings. Though reinforced with concrete over time, its original warmth and layout remain intact.

🌟 Fun Fact: The backyard once held a piggery with 50 pigs—making this coastal home not just historic, but incredibly hardworking, too!



Location: Gloria Ancestral House
