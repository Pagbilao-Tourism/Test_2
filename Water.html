Iringan Falls



Photo taken April of 2016
by Angel Juarez

The trail to Iringan Falls starts on a dirt road left of Maharlika Highway, just after Iringan Bridge. A two-kilometer mix of trail and river trekking leads adventurers through a scenic route. Near a river junction, a right turn and a short walk past a smaller cascade reveals Iringan Falls‚Äîa single-layer, horsetail-type waterfall dropping from about 15.24 meters into a shallow, crystal-clear basin. The catch basin is around 1.8 meters wide and only knee-deep for the average Filipino. Surrounded by layered sedimentary rocks perfect for climbing, the base is often filled with sharp, jagged stones from years of erosion. The water flows into Iringan River, which eventually joins Pagbilao Bay. The falls are alive with nature‚Äîcroaking frogs, chirping birds, and rustling lizards hidden in dense foliage. The cool air and soothing sounds offer a peaceful escape, though visitors should watch for leeches blending in with the rocks. Iringan Falls, with its intense flow and glass-like waters, is both a visual and sensory treat for nature lovers.

Kamaluya Falls



Photo taken October 2021
LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Kamaluya Falls is a stunning three-tier cascade towering at around 200 feet, located deep within Brgy. Ilaya Bagumbungan. Reaching it takes a challenging four-hour trek from the barangay hall, but the reward is worth the effort. The area is rich in plant life, with peace lilies (*Spathiphyllum*), bamboo, mahogany, and apitong trees lining the trail. Massive balete roots cling to the rocks, adding to the wild, untouched charm. At the base, a plunge pool about 3 feet deep and 1.43 meters wide forms, though its size and flow vary with rainfall‚Äîmaking every visit a unique experience.

Amao Falls



Photo taken February of 2022
LGU Pagbilao Tourism

Amao Falls gets its name from Sitio Amao, the nearest area in Silangang Malicboy. Just a kilometer away‚Äîor about a 20-minute trek‚Äîfrom the DENR Region IV-A PASu office, the trail to the falls begins right behind the information center. Along the way, hikers can spot diverse flora like guyabano (Annona muricata), rattan, niyog, niyog-niyugan, and the bay-it tree. Before reaching the base of the majestic eight-tier waterfall, visitors must climb a 4-meter-high boulder for the best view. Each tier of the cascade is spaced roughly 8 meters apart. The largest plunge pool at the bottom spans about 11.28 meters wide and reaches up to 1.5 meters deep. Just above it, the second plunge is about 6 meters wide with an estimated depth of 10 meters. The falls are framed by rich forest vegetation, home to a variety of plant species such as the tibig (Ficus nota), balete (Ficus benjamina), malapapaya, lamio (Dracontomelon dao), tambo, and als-as (Pandanus luzonensis), making it not only a natural wonder but a biodiversity haven.

Altar Mayor



Photo taken October 2021
by Cruz, Marko

Altar Mayor is a moss-covered rock formation in Brgy. Ikirin, rising 10‚Äì15 meters above the water‚Äîand, according to legend, plunging just as deep below. Locals whisper of a hidden underwater tunnel that leads to a distant church in either Pagbilao or Tayabas, depending on who tells the tale. Its name comes from stories of apparitions of the Virgin Mary and secret masses once held by Spanish priests atop the rock. In earlier times, the site was used for bathing and laundry, but today it's mostly avoided. Stories of ghostly voices, towering shadow figures, and sudden unexplained illnesses keep most people away. A riprap once built by a former mayor to protect the site has mysteriously vanished‚Äîjust one more piece of the puzzle surrounding this legendary landmark.

Palsabangon River



Photo taken January 2022
LGU Tourism Pagbilao

The Palsabangon River flows gracefully through Brgy. Ilayang Palsabangon before merging with Tayabas Bay. Fed by the majestic Palsabangon Falls, its clear waters reveal vibrant river rocks and are lined with loamy soil, dense greenery, and a charming hanging bridge that connects local communities. Steeped in folklore, stories speak of ghost ships carrying gold, vanishing villagers, and enchanted wild pigs. During WWII, the river also played a key role in local resistance against Japanese forces. Today, it serves many purposes‚Äîfrom sacred rituals and family picnics to supporting industrial needs like Pagbilao‚Äôs power plant.

üåü Fun Fact: Some believe the river‚Äôs sudden floods are caused by a ghostly gold ship that still roams beneath the surface!



Location: Palsabangon River
